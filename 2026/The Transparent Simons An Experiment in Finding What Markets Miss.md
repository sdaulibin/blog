## From a PhD thesis on multi-source financial forecasting to a daily AI-powered virtual trading experiment

In 2010, I published a paper at IEEE ICDM called "Financial Forecasting with Gompertz Multiple Kernel Learning." The core idea was simple: instead of trying to predict a stock market index using only its own historical data (depth), I combined data from five major international indexes simultaneously (breadth). The result was clear. Breadth beat depth. A model that saw across multiple markets outperformed models that looked deeper into any single market.

That paper also surfaced a harder problem: financial data is non-stationary. The patterns you learn from the past don't hold forever because the underlying distribution keeps changing. My solution was the Gompertz Multiple Kernel Learning model, which gave more weight to recent data and combined information sources in a non-linear way.

Fifteen years later, I find myself doing the same thing, in a completely different domain, with a completely different language. But the logic hasn't changed.

## The idea behind the experiment

I just finished writing a ten-part series called "The Art of Trading," exploring ten legendary traders and investors through a single lens: every one of them saw something in the market that others couldn't see, built a system around that insight, and then got trapped by the system they built.

The series spans a century, from Jesse Livermore to Warren Buffett, from Shanghai to Wall Street, from individual tragedy to collective failure. The common structure is always the same: insight becomes system, system becomes rigidity, rigidity meets reality, and the gap between them produces either renewal or collapse.

That "gap" is the key concept.

Every market price is the sum of all participants' understanding of the world. That understanding is always incomplete. The places where it is most rigid, where consensus is strongest, where everyone believes the same thing, are exactly where the gap between understanding and reality is widest.

This is not just an intuition. It has a formal mathematical basis.

In a series of papers I have been developing called ZFCρ, I establish a meta-theoretic result: for any formal system that attempts to fully characterize a domain, there exists a structurally inevitable, non-empty remainder that the system cannot capture. This remainder, denoted ρ, is not a failure of the system. It is a necessary consequence of formalization itself. The founding paper, "On the Remainder of Choice: A Meta-Theoretic Thesis on ZFC, with a Concrete Realization via the Ramanujan 1/π Formula Factory" (Qin, 2025; [DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.18914682](https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.18914682)), proves this at the level of set theory and demonstrates it concretely through the structure of Ramanujan-type series.

Applied to markets: every participant's model of the market is a formalization. Every formalization produces a non-empty remainder. The collective market price is the superposition of all these formalizations, and the collective remainder is the gap this experiment seeks to find. The remainder is not random noise. It is structured, identifiable, and generated by the very act of formalization. It cannot be eliminated, only shifted. This is why gaps are permanent features of markets, not temporary inefficiencies that get arbitraged away.

Jim Simons at Renaissance Technologies built the most successful hedge fund in history by finding these gaps in market microstructure. His Medallion Fund averaged 66% annual returns over three decades. But his method was completely closed. No one knows what his algorithms do. And for good reason: if his trading were visible, competitors would replicate it and the gaps he exploits would disappear.

This experiment does the opposite.

I call it "Transparent Simons."

## Why transparency is possible

Simons had to be secretive because his trades moved markets. His fund's volume was large enough to change the very prices he was trading on. Visibility would kill his edge.

This experiment uses a virtual portfolio. No real money, no market impact, no reflexivity. My operations don't change any price. So I can show everything: what gap I identify each day, why I think it's a gap, what I trade, and whether I'm right or wrong.

The entire decision-making process is public.

## Why zero human judgment

Here's the part that surprises people: I don't make the trading decisions. Claude does.

I have no special ability to read markets. What I do have is a theoretical framework that says gaps between collective understanding and reality are structurally inevitable, identifiable, and exploitable. If that theory is correct, it should work even when the person executing it has no trading expertise, as long as the executor can reason across multiple domains simultaneously.

Claude is arguably the best cross-domain reasoning engine that currently exists. It can process price data, macroeconomic indicators, earnings call sentiment, geopolitical developments, and market narratives in a single chain of thought. It doesn't suffer from fear, greed, ego, or the need to be right. It can find gaps at the intersection of domains that no single-domain expert would notice.

This is exactly the breadth-over-depth approach from my PhD work, scaled up by several orders of magnitude.

If the experiment works over time, it validates not my judgment, but the underlying theory. If it doesn't work, that's equally valuable data.

## The rules

The rules are deliberately simple:

- **Starting capital**: $10,000 (virtual)
- **Market**: US equities
- **Execution**: Daily closing price
- **Frequency**: At most one operation per day, which may include multiple buys and sells
- **No operation** is always an option. "No clear gap today" is a valid judgment.
- **Position sizing**: Determined by the clarity of the gap signal. No fixed rule. The AI decides.
- **Holding period**: Completely free
- **Transaction costs**: Not considered
- **Full transparency**: Every day's market assessment, gap identification, trading logic, current holdings, and cumulative P&L are published

No hindsight editing. No selective reporting. Right is right, wrong is wrong.

## Connection to the PhD work

My 2010 paper showed that Multiple Kernel Learning, which should theoretically outperform single-source models, actually failed on financial data because of non-stationarity. The market's structure changes over time, and a model trained on the past doesn't automatically work on the future.

The Transparent Simons experiment faces the same challenge. The gaps I'm looking for are produced by the market's current collective understanding. That understanding changes daily. Yesterday's rigidity might be today's flexibility. A consensus that was rock-solid last week might shatter after a single earnings call.

This is why the experiment runs daily, with fresh analysis each time. It doesn't carry forward a fixed model. Each day is a new assessment of where the market's understanding is most rigid right now.

In my PhD, I addressed non-stationarity by giving more weight to recent data (the Gompertz function). In this experiment, Claude addresses it by having no attachment to yesterday's thesis. Every day starts clean.

## What this is not

This is not investment advice. I don't recommend anyone trade real money based on these outputs. This is a theory validation experiment, nothing more.

It's also not a claim that AI can "beat the market." It's a specific test of a specific idea: that the remainder produced by any formalization of market reality, as established in ZFCρ, is structurally inevitable and can be identified through cross-domain reasoning.
